Warning Signs to Watch For

Recognizing early signs — in yourself or someone you care about — is the first and most important step toward getting the right help.

Signs & Symptoms

What to Watch For

These signs are not weaknesses — they are signals from your mind that it needs care. No single sign confirms a condition, but patterns over time are worth taking seriously.

Emotional & Cognitive Signs

Persistent sadness, hopelessness, or emptiness that doesn't lift after several weeks

Extreme mood swings — uncontrollable highs and lows that disrupt daily life

Excessive worry, fear, or feeling constantly on edge without clear cause

Difficulty concentrating, remembering things, or making even simple decisions

Thoughts of self-harm or feelings that life is not worth living

Feeling detached from reality, experiencing dissociation, or persistent confusion

Intense feelings of guilt, shame, or worthlessness that feel disproportionate

Behavioral & Physical Signs

Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities you once found meaningful or enjoyable

Significant changes in sleep — far too much or struggling to sleep at all

Notable changes in appetite or weight without an intentional dietary change

Increased use of alcohol, drugs, or other substances as a way to cope

Unexplained physical symptoms like frequent headaches, stomach aches, or fatigue

Declining performance at work, school, or in daily responsibilities

Neglecting personal hygiene, appearance, or basic self-care for extended periods

Signs in Children & Adolescents

Sudden drop in school performance or persistent refusal to attend school

Frequent tantrums, outbursts, or disproportionate emotional responses

Excessive clinginess or intense fear of separation from parents or caregivers

Loss of interest in play, hobbies, or spending time with friends

Regressive behaviors such as bedwetting in older children

Signs in Older Adults

Increased social isolation or withdrawal from previously enjoyed activities

Noticeable changes in memory, judgment, or ability to manage daily tasks

Expressing feelings of being a burden to family or friends

Giving away valued possessions or making unusual end-of-life comments

Unexplained physical decline or changes in medication adherence

Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US)

If you or someone is in crisis right now:

Contact a crisis helpline immediately. Dial 988 in the US, or reach out to your local emergency services or mental health crisis line. You don't have to face a crisis alone.

Taking Action

What to Do If You Notice These Signs

01
Don't Dismiss It

Take what you're observing seriously. Trusting your instincts when something feels wrong is a strength, not an overreaction.

02
Talk Openly

If it's someone else, approach them with genuine curiosity and compassion — not judgment. Ask directly how they're feeling.

03
Seek Professional Help

Encourage a visit to a doctor, therapist, or mental health professional who can properly assess the situation.

04
Stay Connected

Don't disappear after the first conversation. Regular check-ins and emotional presence matter enormously in recovery.
